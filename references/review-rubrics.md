# Review Rubrics

Use these rubrics when reviewing a draft.

## Thesis Clarity

5: The central claim is unmistakable and appears early enough.
3: The thesis is present but diluted or late.
1: The piece has a topic but no clear claim.

Checks:

- Is the thesis explicit in the first 1 to 3 paragraphs?
- Is the thesis arguable rather than merely descriptive?
- Does the thesis explain why the topic matters now?

## Audience Fit

5: The draft meets the audience at the right knowledge level and addresses likely objections.
3: The draft mostly fits but overexplains, underexplains, or misses some objections.
1: The draft appears written for a different reader.

## Persona Consistency

5: Voice, posture, vocabulary, and tone match `PERSONA.md`.
3: Mostly consistent with occasional generic or off-tone sections.
1: The persona is not visible.

## Evidence Quality

5: Major claims are supported at the required proof standard.
3: Some important claims need stronger support or clearer attribution.
1: The draft relies on assertion where evidence is required.

Checks:

- Are strong evidence and illustrative evidence separated?
- Does any paragraph carry more certainty than the evidence justifies?
- Are assumptions, trend extrapolations, and facts labeled clearly?
- Is opposing evidence acknowledged where it materially affects the argument?

## Structure

5: The argument progresses naturally and each section earns its place.
3: The structure works but has redundancy, weak transitions, or misplaced material.
1: The draft is hard to follow.

Checks:

- Does each section advance a claim, evidence point, objection, or implication?
- Does the opening avoid broad generic framing?
- Does the draft move the reader from current belief to intended belief?

## Style And Rhythm

5: Sentences are varied, readable, and appropriate for the publication context.
3: Clear but uneven or repetitive.
1: Flat, generic, awkward, or bloated.

Checks:

- Are claims visible and easy to scan?
- Are paragraphs tight, with one strong idea per paragraph?
- Are acronyms and specialized terms explained unless common to the audience?

## Mechanism Quality

5: The paper explains how and why the recommendation works, including constraints and failure modes.
3: Mechanisms are present but thin, implied, or uneven.
1: The draft recommends without explaining the operating logic.

Checks:

- Does the paper explain mechanism, not just recommendation?
- Are cost, complexity, organizational impact, execution risk, and time-to-value explicit where relevant?
- Are technology choices connected to operating model or business implications?

## Decision Usefulness

5: A senior stakeholder would know what to do, fund, approve, rethink, or discuss next.
3: The paper is informative but weak on implications or decisions.
1: The reader finishes without clear action or decision relevance.

Checks:

- Would a senior stakeholder know what to do with this?
- Are recommendations, implications, or next steps concrete?
- Are trade-offs visible enough to support a decision?

## Technical Credibility

5: A technical reader would respect the reasoning and not feel talked down to.
3: Mostly credible but has loose terminology, unsupported leaps, or shallow mechanisms.
1: Generic, hype-driven, or technically thin.

Checks:

- Would a technical reader respect the reasoning?
- Are important constraints and caveats included?
- Does the draft avoid AI hype, vendor language, and inflated abstractions?

## Publication Readiness

5: Ready after light copyedit.
3: Needs one substantive revision.
1: Needs rethinking or major rewrite.
