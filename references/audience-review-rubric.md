# Audience Review Rubric

Use this fixed seven-dimension rubric when scoring outlines or drafts for audience fit.

Score each dimension from 1 to 5.

- **5:** Strong. Works for the selected audience with little or no change.
- **4:** Good. Minor improvement would sharpen audience fit.
- **3:** Adequate. Understandable, but likely misses a reader need or creates friction.
- **2:** Weak. The audience will struggle, object, distrust, or not know what to do.
- **1:** Failing. The artifact is aimed at the wrong reader or does not serve the audience.

For every score of 3 or below, produce an actionable rewrite instruction. The instruction must say what to change, where, and why. Do not provide vague criticism.

## Fixed Dimensions

### 1. Thesis Clarity

Does the reader understand the core claim quickly?

Low-score triggers:

- thesis is missing, buried, descriptive, or too broad
- opening delays the point
- reader cannot say what the paper argues

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Move the thesis into [location] and rewrite it as a claim: "[claim shape]".

### 2. Audience Relevance

Does the draft speak to what this audience cares about?

Low-score triggers:

- does not connect to reader's role, incentives, pain, or decision context
- focuses on author interest instead of reader need
- assumes the reader cares before earning it

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Add a sentence in [section] that connects [claim] to [audience concern/outcome].

### 3. Evidence Sufficiency

Does the draft meet the audience's proof standard?

Low-score triggers:

- claims lack evidence
- evidence is illustrative when the audience needs proof
- certainty exceeds source strength
- opposing evidence is ignored

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Support [claim] with [type of evidence] or reduce certainty to [caveated form].

### 4. Objection Handling

Does the draft handle the objections that would block acceptance?

Low-score triggers:

- likely objections are absent
- counterarguments are straw-manned
- trade-offs are hidden
- risks are underplayed

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Add a short objection-and-response paragraph after [section] addressing [objection].

### 5. Jargon Appropriateness

Is the technical or domain language right for this audience?

Low-score triggers:

- unexplained acronyms
- insider language without payoff
- overexplaining basics to specialists
- removing technical detail needed for trust

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Define [term] on first use for [audience], or remove it if it does not change the argument.

### 6. Decision Usefulness

Does the reader know what to do, decide, fund, challenge, or rethink?

Low-score triggers:

- recommendations are vague
- implications are missing
- ownership/timing is unclear
- no ask or next step

Actionable instruction pattern:

> End [section/paper] with a concrete implication: [decision/action/next step].

### 7. Structural Flow

Does the paper move the reader from current belief to intended shift without avoidable friction?

Low-score triggers:

- sections are out of order
- claims repeat
- evidence appears before the reader knows why it matters
- conclusion does not follow from body

Actionable instruction pattern:

> Reorder [sections] so the reader sees [problem] before [recommendation/evidence].

## Mode Guidance

### Lite

Use for early outlines, rough briefs, or fast checks.

Output:

- score table
- top 3 audience-fit failures
- one rewrite instruction per low score
- go/no-go recommendation for drafting

Do not do a full reverse outline.

### Deep

Use for late-stage drafts.

Output:

- score table
- reverse outline
- objection map
- decision-gap analysis
- stop-reading points
- actionable rewrite instructions for every low score

Deep mode should still stay concise. Do not line-edit.

## Non-Negotiable Constraints

- Do not praise.
- Do not line-edit.
- Do not rewrite unless asked.
- Focus on audience fit and decision readiness.
- Use concise, operational feedback.
- For every low score, provide an actionable rewrite instruction.
