# Audience Profile

This profile defines who the piece is for and how the writing should adapt to them.

## Audience Boundary

This profile describes the reader model, objections, proof standard, desired reader shift, and paper-specific adaptation rules. It does not define interactive TUI behavior, file snapshot policy, feedback approval mechanics, or workflow gates.

## Audience Source

- **Mode:** Hybrid.
- **Curated personas used:** cxo-reader; distinguished-architect-engineer.
- **Custom audience defined:** Yes.
- **Persona improvements made before use:** Added regulated-enterprise operating context, technology-domain ownership concerns, and federation / control trade-offs.
- **Primary audience priority:** Senior executive sponsor wins when needs conflict.

## Audience Set

Use this section when the paper has multiple audiences.

| Audience | Priority | Why They Matter | Success Condition |
|----------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Senior executive sponsor | Primary | Can validate the direction, sponsor pilots, and shape coalition framing. | Understands the decision ask, why now, pilot path, and governance risk. |
| Peer architecture leaders | Secondary | Need enough operating detail to pressure-test the model and co-own the next conversation. | Can see the ownership model, architecture role, and where the proposal needs evidence. |
| Control / risk partners | Tertiary | Claims touch resilience, security, third-party, model, and operational risk. | See that the paper respects evidence burden and avoids unsupported regulatory overreach. |

## Reader

- **Primary audience:** Senior executive sponsor accountable for major technology, data, procurement, or platform decisions.
- **Role/context:** Executive reviewing whether to validate a strategic direction before broader peer conversations and pilot planning.
- **Expertise level:** Executive with strong operating context; not looking for deep implementation detail in the opening.
- **Time/patience:** Skim first, deeper read only if the ask and stakes are crisp.

## Current Beliefs

They likely believe:

- The technology estate is already managed through existing procurement, architecture, risk, and operating controls.
- Federated technology decisions are necessary, but they can create inconsistent standards and duplicated investment.
- New governance language is cheap; proof requires operating mechanisms, ownership, and measurable signals.

## Needs

They need:

- A clear decision ask and pilot path.
- A concrete explanation of why current mechanisms are insufficient.
- Confidence that the proposal does not create another approval bureaucracy.
- Enough evidence standard to know what must be researched or proven before broader circulation.

## Objections

They may object that:

- This sounds like governance expansion under a new label.
- Domain ownership may be politically difficult or already covered by existing roles.
- Live telemetry is not mature enough to drive decisions.
- The proposal mixes strategy, architecture, operating model, and controls into one paper.
- Regulatory and security claims may be too strong without citations.

## Desired Shift

After reading, they should:

- **Understand:** Lifecycle management is about operated decisions after adoption, not just intake governance.
- **Believe:** A pilot is justified if it starts with named ownership, minimum viable signals, and narrow domain scope.
- **Do:** Validate the position, sponsor a pilot path, and approve coalition framing for peer architecture conversations.

## Adaptation Rules

- **Context depth:** Medium. Explain enough context to make the problem concrete, but move quickly to the decision model.
- **Technical depth:** Moderate. Use operating mechanisms and examples, not implementation detail.
- **Proof standard:** Claims about regulation, security, operational resilience, and cost require source mapping before final draft.
- **Tone fit:** Direct, executive, pragmatic, and risk-aware.
- **Conflict rule:** Executive decision usefulness beats architecture completeness; put deep operating detail in later sections or appendices.

## Scoring Emphasis

- Decision usefulness.
- Evidence sufficiency.
- Objection handling.
- Thesis clarity.
- Jargon appropriateness.
