# Outline

## Mode

- **Depth:** Lite
- **Output:** outline_only

## Structure Verdict

Ready to draft.

## Recommended Structure

Use a short status-update structure: current state, blocker, ask, next checkpoint. This fits the low-risk internal update job and avoids unnecessary research or strategy-paper scaffolding.

## Reader Journey

- **Starting belief:** The sponsor believes the work is broadly on track but may not know whether action is needed.
- **Target belief:** The sponsor knows the work is on track and must confirm one owner this week.
- **Core shift:** Move from passive status awareness to a specific owner-confirmation action.
- **Core tension:** The update must avoid sounding like an escalation while still making the dependency visible.

## Outline

### 1. Status And Ask

- **Objective:** Give the status and owner-confirmation ask immediately.
- **Reader state in:** The reader wants to know whether anything needs action.
- **Reader state out:** The reader knows the work is on track and one owner must be confirmed.
- **Reader job:** Decide whether to confirm or assign the owner.
- **Main claim:** Platform readiness is on track if the owner is confirmed this week.
- **Supporting points:** Preparation complete; dependency visible; ask is narrow.
- **Evidence:** Internal team status.
- **Evidence strength:** not evidence-dependent
- **Reader questions addressed:** Are we on track? What do you need?
- **Objection handled:** Is this an escalation?
- **Why here:** Status updates should not bury the ask.
- **Decision implication:** Sponsor confirms owner.
- **Approximate length:** 1-2 paragraphs
- **Transition to next:** Explain what changed.
- **Drafting notes:** Keep concise.

### 2. What Changed

- **Objective:** Summarize progress since the last update.
- **Reader state in:** The reader knows the ask but needs confidence the work is moving.
- **Reader state out:** The reader sees concrete progress.
- **Reader job:** Understand why broad escalation is not needed.
- **Main claim:** Preparation moved forward enough for the next checkpoint.
- **Supporting points:** Readiness checklist refreshed; checkpoint materials prepared; dependency list narrowed.
- **Evidence:** Internal delivery state.
- **Evidence strength:** not evidence-dependent
- **Reader questions addressed:** What changed?
- **Objection handled:** Is the update too optimistic?
- **Why here:** Progress context makes the blocker easier to interpret.
- **Decision implication:** Keep the work moving.
- **Approximate length:** 2-3 bullets
- **Transition to next:** Name the blocker.
- **Drafting notes:** Avoid broad background.

### 3. Blocker And Next Checkpoint

- **Objective:** Name the blocker, owner action, and checkpoint.
- **Reader state in:** The reader sees progress but needs the risk framed.
- **Reader state out:** The reader knows what must happen before the next checkpoint.
- **Reader job:** Confirm owner and attend checkpoint if needed.
- **Main claim:** The blocker is manageable if ownership is confirmed this week.
- **Supporting points:** Owner confirmation; checkpoint timing; escalation condition if ownership slips.
- **Evidence:** Internal dependency state.
- **Evidence strength:** not evidence-dependent
- **Reader questions addressed:** What happens next? When does this escalate?
- **Objection handled:** What if the owner is not confirmed?
- **Why here:** The close should turn status into action.
- **Decision implication:** Confirm owner before checkpoint.
- **Approximate length:** 2-3 bullets plus closing line
- **Transition to next:** End.
- **Drafting notes:** End with concrete date-free checkpoint language.

## Thesis Placement

The thesis appears in the opening paragraph.

## Objection Map

| Objection | Where Addressed | Handling |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| This sounds like escalation. | Status And Ask | State that work remains on track and the ask is narrow. |
| The owner gap may be larger than stated. | Blocker And Next Checkpoint | Name an escalation condition if ownership slips. |

## Cut / Park List

- Roadmap strategy -- out of scope for a weekly update.
- Architecture background -- not needed for current action.
- External evidence -- not relevant to current status.

## Drafting Risks

- Burying the ask.
- Over-explaining background.
- Naming real people or systems.
