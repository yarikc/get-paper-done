# Audience Profile

This profile defines who the piece is for and how the writing should adapt to them.

## Audience Source

- **Mode:** Prioritized multi.
- **Curated personas used:** None.
- **Custom audience defined:** Yes.
- **Persona improvements made before use:** Added external proof expectations and risk-reader skepticism.
- **Primary audience priority:** Senior technology, data, and AI leaders.

## Audience Set

| Audience | Priority | Why They Matter | Success Condition |
|----------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Senior technology, data, and AI leaders | Primary | They decide whether governance becomes operating discipline or presentation material. | They see a practical control-evidence model they can adapt. |
| Risk, compliance, and audit partners | Secondary | They challenge public claims and evidence quality. | They see caveats, control ownership, and evidence boundaries. |
| Technical practitioners | Tertiary | They build and maintain the controls. | They understand what signals and artifacts the model requires. |

## Reader

- **Primary audience:** Senior technology, data, and AI leaders.
- **Role/context:** Leaders shaping AI governance, delivery controls, and public trust posture.
- **Expertise level:** Familiar with AI governance language, unevenly familiar with operating-control implementation.
- **Time/patience:** Moderate; they will read if the argument is concrete and avoids slogans.

## Current Beliefs

They likely believe:

- Responsible AI principles are necessary but not sufficient.
- External claims need defensible evidence.
- Control-heavy programs can slow delivery if poorly designed.
- Existing risk, security, data, and model governance processes may already cover part of the problem.

## Needs

They need:

- A clear distinction between principles and control evidence.
- A practical model for evidence that can survive scrutiny.
- A fair treatment of delivery-speed concerns.
- Boundaries around what the evidence can and cannot prove.

## Objections

They may object that:

- The paper turns responsible AI into bureaucracy.
- Existing governance controls already cover the need.
- Synthetic examples are too abstract.
- Public-facing claims should avoid detailed control discussion.

## Desired Shift

After reading, they should:

- **Understand:** Responsible AI credibility depends on observable operating evidence.
- **Believe:** A small set of well-owned controls is more credible than broad principle statements.
- **Do:** Evaluate their own AI claims against control ownership, evidence quality, exception handling, and review cadence.

## Adaptation Rules

- **Context depth:** Medium.
- **Technical depth:** Medium.
- **Proof standard:** Source-backed strategic judgment with clear caveats.
- **Tone fit:** External, precise, and non-promotional.
- **Conflict rule:** Public credibility beats rhetorical simplicity; delivery practicality beats exhaustive control catalogs.

## Scoring Emphasis

- Evidence discipline.
- Caveat quality.
- Audience conflict handling.
- Practical mechanism.
- No unsupported legal or regulatory claims.
