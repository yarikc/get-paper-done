# Distinguished Architect / Engineer

Use this curated audience persona for senior technical leaders, principal engineers, distinguished engineers, and architecture review audiences.

## Reader

- **Primary audience:** Distinguished architect, principal engineer, senior platform engineer, architecture fellow, or deeply technical leader.
- **Role/context:** Evaluates whether the argument is technically coherent, implementable, bounded, and honest about alternatives.
- **Expertise level:** Specialist.
- **Time/patience:** Will read deeply if mechanisms are credible; will disengage quickly from hand-waving.

## Current Beliefs

They likely believe:

- Many executive technology papers oversimplify the hard parts.
- The real value is in boundaries, failure modes, and trade-offs.
- Claims need mechanisms, not slogans.

## Needs

They need:

- Mechanism: how the recommendation works.
- Boundaries: where it applies and where it does not.
- Alternatives: what else was considered and why rejected.
- Technical coherence: definitions, dependencies, architecture implications, and failure modes.

## Objections

They may object that:

- The draft asserts technical conclusions without mechanism.
- The terms are vague or overloaded.
- Alternatives and failure modes are missing.
- The recommendation ignores platform constraints or operational realities.

## Desired Shift

After reading, they should:

- **Understand:** The mechanism, boundaries, and architecture implications.
- **Believe:** The argument is technically coherent and operationally credible.
- **Do:** Engage, refine, sponsor technical design, or stop a flawed direction early.

## Adaptation Rules

- **Context depth:** Medium. Avoid basics, but define ambiguous terms.
- **Technical depth:** High. Mechanisms, dependencies, boundaries, and alternatives matter.
- **Proof standard:** Technical reasoning, concrete examples, evidence, constraints, and failure modes.
- **Tone fit:** Precise, sober, technically fluent, low fluff.

## Draft Scoring Emphasis

- Mechanism quality
- Technical coherence
- Boundary clarity
- Alternative evaluation
- Jargon appropriateness
