# CxO Reader

Use this curated audience persona for chief-level executives and enterprise decision makers.

## Reader

- **Primary audience:** CEO, CIO, CTO, CDAO, CDO, or other CxO-level decision maker.
- **Role/context:** Owns strategic direction, investment choices, risk posture, and cross-organization execution.
- **Expertise level:** Executive; may understand technology direction but will not tolerate implementation sprawl.
- **Time/patience:** Skim first, then selective deep read if the strategic value is obvious.

## Current Beliefs

They likely believe:

- Technology papers often overfocus on mechanisms and understate business consequences.
- Every proposal competes with other investment, risk, and execution priorities.
- Strategy without execution logic is not actionable.

## Needs

They need:

- Strategic clarity: what problem matters and why now.
- Investment logic: what is worth funding, sequencing, or stopping.
- Trade-offs: cost, risk, timing, complexity, and organizational impact.
- Execution risk: what could fail and how it will be governed.

## Objections

They may object that:

- The paper is technically interesting but not decision-useful.
- The recommendation lacks a credible path to execution.
- The investment case is unclear or too broad.
- Risks and dependencies are understated.

## Desired Shift

After reading, they should:

- **Understand:** The strategic problem, why it matters now, and what decision is needed.
- **Believe:** The recommendation has credible business and execution logic.
- **Do:** Approve, fund, challenge, sequence, or sponsor the next move.

## Adaptation Rules

- **Context depth:** Low to medium. Lead with strategic frame; add technical context only when it changes the decision.
- **Technical depth:** Mechanism-light but not shallow. Explain enough to establish credibility.
- **Proof standard:** Named evidence, strategic consequences, risk logic, and credible execution path.
- **Tone fit:** Direct, concise, executive-readable, no hype, no consultant slogans.

## Draft Scoring Emphasis

- Decision usefulness
- Trade-off clarity
- Investment logic
- Execution risk
- Structural flow
